Digital PCR vs Real-Time PCR: Technology Comparison and Application Guide

Digital PCR (dPCR) has emerged as a powerful alternative to real-time qPCR for applications requiring absolute quantification. But dPCR is not a replacement — each technology has distinct strengths. Here is how to choose.

What is the difference between digital PCR and real-time PCR (qPCR)?

Digital PCR (dPCR) partitions the sample into thousands of individual reactions to count absolute DNA copies without standard curves, while qPCR uses fluorescence monitoring during amplification and requires standard curves for quantification. dPCR offers higher precision for low-abundance targets.

How Each Technology Works

Real-time PCR (qPCR) measures fluorescence accumulation during amplification. Quantification is relative — the cycle threshold (Ct) value is compared to a standard curve or normalized to a reference gene (delta-delta-Ct). The signal is analog and depends on amplification efficiency.

Digital PCR (dPCR) partitions the sample into thousands (chip-based) or millions (droplet-based) of individual reactions. Each partition contains 0 or 1 target molecules. After endpoint PCR, partitions are counted as positive or negative. Quantification is absolute — the number of target molecules is calculated from the Poisson distribution of positive partitions. No standard curve is needed.

When to Choose qPCR

  • High-throughput gene expression: qPCR remains the standard for 96- and 384-well plate gene expression studies due to speed and cost.
  • Relative quantification: For fold-change analysis (treated vs. untreated), qPCR with delta-delta-Ct is well-established and validated.
  • Wide dynamic range: qPCR covers 7-9 logs, making it suitable for samples with highly variable target concentrations.
  • Existing protocols: Thousands of validated qPCR assays are published. Converting to dPCR requires re-validation.

When to Choose dPCR

  • Absolute quantification: When you need exact copies/µL without a standard curve — e.g., viral load quantification, transgene copy number.
  • Rare target detection: dPCR detects mutations at 0.1% allele frequency vs. 1-5% for qPCR. Ideal for liquid biopsy and minimal residual disease.
  • Inhibitor-prone samples: Environmental samples, FFPE DNA, and blood samples often contain PCR inhibitors. dPCR's endpoint readout is more tolerant.
  • Copy number variation (CNV): dPCR provides precise absolute CNV measurements without a reference standard.
  • NGS library quantification: dPCR is the gold standard for quantifying NGS libraries before sequencing.

Cost Analysis for Indian Labs

For Indian research labs, the cost differential is significant. A qPCR instrument (Bio-Rad CFX96 or equivalent) costs Rs 15-25 lakh. A dPCR system (Bio-Rad QX200 or equivalent) costs Rs 40-70 lakh. Per-reaction costs for dPCR are 2-3x higher — Rs 400-1,200 vs Rs 150-400 for qPCR.

However, for specific applications like liquid biopsy, the cost per actionable result may favor dPCR because its higher sensitivity reduces the number of replicate reactions needed.

Emerging Technologies: dPCR Is Evolving

New dPCR platforms are closing the gap with qPCR:

  • Naica (Stilla): Crystal digital PCR partitions samples into 30,000 droplets in a 2D monolayer for easier imaging.
  • QIAcuity (Qiagen): Nanoplate-based dPCR with 26,000 partitions per well, reducing run time to 90 minutes.
  • Digital droplet PCR (ddPCR): Bio-Rad's QX600 supports 6-color multiplexing, matching qPCR's multiplex capability.
