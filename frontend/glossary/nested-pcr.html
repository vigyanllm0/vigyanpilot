nested PCR

PCR & Amplification

Definition

Nested PCR uses two primer sets in two rounds (outer then inner/nested primers) to dramatically increase specificity: the first round amplifies broadly, and the second round selectively amplifies the target within the first amplicon, ideal for rare targets.

Mechanism / How It Works

Nested PCR is a two-stage amplification strategy designed to dramatically improve specificity and sensitivity for detecting low-abundance targets. In the first round (outer PCR, 20–30 cycles), an external primer pair amplifies a larger fragment (typically 500–1500 bp) containing the target region. In the second round (inner PCR, 25–35 cycles), a second primer pair (the "nested" primers) binds within the product of the first reaction, amplifying a shorter internal fragment (typically 100–400 bp). The nested primers require that the first-round product contained the correct internal sequence — any nonspecific product from the first round that lacks the internal target region cannot be amplified in the second round. This two-tier specificity provides a 10³- to 10⁶-fold increase in selectivity compared to single-round PCR. Each round uses 1–2 μL of the previous reaction product (or 1:10–1:100 dilution) as template, minimizing carryover of primers and other components. A variation, semi-nested PCR, uses one outer primer and one inner primer in the second round for additional flexibility. The total amplification power approaches 2^(n₁+n₂) theoretically, though practical yields are limited by dNTP and polymerase availability. Nested PCR can detect as few as 1–10 target copies in complex DNA backgrounds containing up to 1 μg of genomic DNA.

Applications in Research

Nested PCR is the method of choice for detecting low-copy-number pathogens such as HIV-1 proviral DNA, hepatitis B virus in seronegative individuals, and Mycobacterium tuberculosis in paucibacillary specimens. In archaeological and paleogenetic studies, nested PCR amplifies ancient DNA (aDNA) from Neanderthal bones and mummified tissues, where template is highly fragmented and present at 0.1–1% of total extractable DNA. In molecular oncology, nested PCR detects minimal residual disease (MRD) in leukemia by amplifying rearranged immunoglobulin (IgH) or T-cell receptor (TCR) gene sequences with sensitivities of 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁶ (one leukemic cell in 10,000–1,000,000 normal cells). In prenatal diagnosis, nested PCR of cell-free fetal DNA from maternal plasma detects fetal RhD status and paternally inherited mutations. In forensic analysis, nested PCR amplifies degraded DNA from touch evidence, bone fragments, and hair shafts where template levels are below 100 pg. In HLA typing, nested PCR amplifies specific allele groups for high-resolution typing at Class I and Class II loci. In 16S rRNA metagenomic analysis, nested PCR increases detection sensitivity for low-abundance bacterial taxa in microbiome studies.

Key Parameters / Variables

Nested PCR parameters include outer amplicon length (300–1500 bp); inner amplicon length (100–350 bp); first-round cycles (20–30); second-round cycles (25–35); first-round annealing temperature (52–58 °C); second-round annealing temperature (55—62 °C); inter-round transfer volume (1–2 μL or 1:10–1:100 dilution); outer primer concentration (0.2–0.5 μM each); inner primer concentration (0.2–0.5 μM each); MgCl₂ concentration (1.5–2 mM); dNTP concentration (200 μM each); and Taq polymerase concentration (0.025–0.05 U/μL per round). Template input for the first round is typically 50–500 ng genomic DNA. For second-round template, 1 μL of a 1:50 dilution of the first-round product avoids excess primer carryover. Sensitivity can reach single-copy detection in 1 μg genomic DNA with optimized conditions.

Common Mistakes / Misconceptions

The most critical error in nested PCR is amplicon carryover contamination: transferring first-round products can aerosolize and contaminate subsequent reactions, producing false positives. Dedicated pre- and post-amplification areas, aerosol-resistant filter tips, and uracil-N-glycosylase (UNG) carryover prevention are essential. Beginners often transfer too much first-round product (>5 μL) into the second round, causing inhibition from leftover primers, dNTPs, or Mg²⁺. Excessive cycles in the first round (35+) can cause plateau-phase artifacts. A false sense of specificity is another pitfall: while nested PCR reduces nonspecific amplification, it can amplify primer-dimer artifacts in both rounds, producing a visible band of the wrong size. Not confirming nested products by sequencing or RFLP analysis can lead to misidentification. Hot-start polymerases are recommended for both rounds to prevent mispriming during reaction setup, especially when the nested primers have partial complementarity to outer primers.
